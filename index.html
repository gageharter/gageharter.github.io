About

A teaching path shaped by science, inclusion, and international classrooms.

My path into science education has taken me from Cambridge Primary classrooms in Cambodia and a Montessori environment in China to secondary biology, special education support, and STEM curriculum leadership in Oregon. Teaching internationally early in my career taught me to be flexible, culturally responsive, and attentive to the different ways students make meaning.

After returning to the United States, I spent two years as a Special Education Skills Trainer supporting students with significant academic, behavioral, communication, and life-skills needs. That experience reshaped how I think about rigor: high expectations matter, but students also need trust, structure, meaningful choices, and multiple ways to demonstrate understanding.

I am drawn to international schools because they unite the parts of teaching I value most—intellectual curiosity, collaboration across cultures, ambitious learning, and genuine community. Outside the classroom, I enjoy building robotics projects and studying aviation and hot-air ballooning, interests that keep me grounded in the joy of learning how systems work.

Teaching Approach

Rigor without exclusion. Inquiry without vagueness.

Students should leave science class able to ask better questions, use evidence, explain systems, and revise their thinking.

01

Start with purpose

A phenomenon, problem, or question gives students a reason to learn the content and connect ideas across scales.

02

Teach strategically

Direct instruction, models, discussion, investigation, and scaffolds are used deliberately—not as competing philosophies.

03

Make thinking visible

Models, data, scientific writing, prototypes, and conversation reveal understanding and help surface misconceptions early.

04

Normalize revision

Students use criteria, evidence, testing, and feedback to improve explanations, investigations, and designs.

Featured Curriculum

Mission Design

A 10-day interdisciplinary STEM systems challenge for grades 6–9.

View Curriculum Overview

Student teams create a world, develop an adapted species and civilization, and respond to a team-specific disruption called the Mission Turn. Every decision must fit the system students have already built: environment, resources, biology, transportation, technology, governance, and values.

There is no single correct solution. Teams are evaluated on system coherence, evidence, iteration, collaboration, and communication.
